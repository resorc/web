RESORC

REsearch Exposure in Socially Relevant Computing

Welcome

The EECS department of Syracuse University is proud to partner with Google Research to launch an undergraduate student research engagement program, RESORC (Research Exposure in SOcially Relevant Computing).

We encourage students from historically-underrepresented groups within computer science to apply, which includes, but is not limited to, women, students of color, first-generation college students, and LGBTQ+ students. No prior research experience required! Our goal is to expose students to what socially relevant computing research can look like, to help them build self-efficacy and become stronger candidates for doctoral programs, and to influence their career choices by providing a supportive community of peers.

About the Program

Who can apply:All current undergraduate students at EECS department can apply. The program will be virtual. However, majority of our sessions will be interactive and synchronous, hence it may be useful to attend the sessions to achieve the maximum benefit.

What experience needed: It is better if the students have completed the introductory computer science course sequence. However, no prior research experience necessary!

How much time commitment: You will need to dedicate 2 days (Saturdays) in December and January to attend the virtual workshops.

Any stipend: Selected participants will receive a merit based stipend. Stipend recipients MUST be US citizen.

What will the program offer:

  • Workshop 1 (December 11, 2021): A virtual workshop on exposure to many research areas of CS, Google Tensorflow workshop and higher education application process and best practices and a keynote by a Googler on state of the art computing innovation.
  • Workshop 2 (January 22, 2022): A virtual workshop on advanced Tensorflow topics, introduction to research methodologies, financing graduate education, preparing REU application, hearing from current graduate students and post-graduate career opportunities.
  • Research opportunity: Tons of research exposure to socially relevant computing based research problems from domain experts and disciplinary pioneers.
  • Networking: Meet and connect and network with faculty and industry researchers and graduate students who are amazing role models to learn from.

    Talk Title: "Federated Learning: Basics, Algorithms, and Tools"

    Talk Title: Packet Routing in Future LEO Satellite Networks
    Abstract: This new decade is bound to witness the deployment of mega-constellations of LEO satellites for the purpose of global Internet services. A number of different constellations are currently being launched (e.g., Starlink, OneWeb, Kuiper, etc.), and there are important differences between them. Two main differences are of interest: the nature of the constellation (star or delta) and whether it features one or more orbital shells (altitude). To provide global Internet services, routing protocols are needed for the exchange of data packets between satellites. To date, many routing protocols have been proposed for satellite networks (SatNets). However, existing protocols are designed for monolithic constellations (i.e., single-shell, delta, or star). In the coming years, cooperation between different types of constellations should be expected, and we should therefore look beyond monolithic constellations in considering routing protocols.

    Talk Title: "Intelligent Cyber-Physical Systems: toward autonomy of computing systems"
    Abstract: Cyber-physical and embedded systems are evolving to incorporate the advancement of AI/machine learning technologies. These systems, unlike general-purpose computing systems, interact with the physical world and face resource constraints, such as size, weight, power, cost, and time. Deeper neural networks require a massive amount of resource capacity to obtain solutions with rigorously refined quality, making them difficult to analyze. Over the ranges of interest, there is a trade-off between solution quality and the resource required to produce the solution. The issue is tackled by designing a deep neural network in a modular way - the framework breaks down this complexity into smaller building blocks, so as to facilitate implementation and maintenance. Hence, when the resources are more constrained the system is constructed with fewer blocks, and vice versa. To this end, the system is designed with adaptive concreteness to produce a feasible answer quickly and to produce an improved answer by performing additional processing as time permits, instead of enforcing strict or predefined requirements for resource/quality. To be sure, facilitating expeditious learning from very large data sets poses a great challenge, especially for real-time prediction and autonomous decision-making.

    Program Goals

  • Train the next generation of scholars to conduct cross-cutting computing research
  • Provide research exposure to undergraduate students from populations that are under-represented in computing by introducing them to research
  • Increase students’ understanding of computing research methodologies
  • Introduce students to graduate education and research career opportunities through preparatory workshops and one-on-one mentoring
  • Provide students with multiple points of support from a diverse group of peers and faculty mentors
